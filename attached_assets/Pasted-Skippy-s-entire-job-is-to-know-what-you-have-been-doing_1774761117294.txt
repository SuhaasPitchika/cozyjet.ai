Skippy's entire job is to know what you have been doing without you having to tell it anything. The concept is that most creators and builders do genuinely interesting work every single day but never think to document or share it because by the time they finish the work they are mentally done and marketing feels like a separate second job. Skippy solves this by connecting to the tools you already use — GitHub, Notion, Figma, Google Drive, Google Calendar — and checking them quietly in the background every two hours like a personal assistant who walks by your desk, sees what you finished, and makes a note of it.
What Skippy actually does when it syncs is read the raw activity data from each connected tool, pass it through a Claude-powered summarization chain, and produce what the product calls a content seed — a clean two to three sentence description of what you did, why it matters professionally, and who on social media would find it interesting. A GitHub commit becomes "You shipped JWT authentication with refresh token support for your SaaS — a security implementation that junior developers and indie founders building their first auth system would find genuinely valuable." A Figma file update becomes "You redesigned your onboarding flow with a focus on reducing time-to-value — a UX decision with a clear rationale that designers and product people would want to hear about." The user never wrote any of that. Skippy read the raw data and turned it into something ready to market.
What Skippy outputs to the dashboard is a live feed of these seeds, newest at the top, each one a card showing the source, the title, the summary, and two buttons: Generate Content which sends it to Meta, and Dismiss which removes it from the feed. The feed is the starting point of every content creation session. When a user opens CozyJet in the morning and sees four seeds already waiting — one from a commit they pushed at eleven PM, one from a Notion page they updated yesterday, one from a calendar event that just ended — the psychological effect is immediate. The work of figuring out what to post is already done. Skippy handled it overnight.

Snooks — The Strategist
Snooks is the agent that thinks about the bigger picture of your content presence rather than any individual post. Where Skippy finds the raw material and Meta writes the actual words, Snooks answers the questions: what should you be posting this week, when should you post it, and is your content calendar balanced or are you accidentally going silent for ten days and then flooding your feed in one afternoon.
The concept behind Snooks is that most solo creators have no content strategy — they post when they remember to and go quiet when life gets busy. A real social media manager would sit down on Monday morning and plan the week, look at what performed well last month, identify gaps, and match the content calendar to trending topics happening right now. Snooks does exactly that automatically. It looks at your Skippy feed to see what raw material exists, checks your recent post analytics to understand what type of content your audience actually engages with, monitors trending topics in the technology and creator space, and then builds a set of weekly suggestions telling you what to post, on which platform, and on which day and time based on when your historical posts have gotten the most traction.
Snooks also manages the calendar itself, showing you a month view where every scheduled post appears as a dot, making gaps visually obvious. Its sticky note style suggestion cards sit beside the calendar as gentle nudges rather than demanding notifications. The framing is important — Snooks never tells you what you must do, it tells you what would work well and lets you decide. This feels like advice from a smart colleague rather than instructions from software.
What Snooks outputs is the weekly suggestion list with titles, platforms, and rationale, the optimal posting time data per platform based on your own historical engagement, the populated content calendar view, and trend alerts when something relevant is gaining momentum in your niche that you could connect your expertise to.

Meta — The Copywriter
Meta is the most visible agent and the one users interact with most directly. Its job is to take a content seed from Skippy, or a raw idea the user types, or a trending topic Snooks flagged, and turn it into ready-to-post content for every social platform simultaneously. The core innovation here is not just that it generates content — every AI tool generates content. The innovation is that it generates content that sounds like the specific user, not like a generic AI assistant, and that it learns to sound more like that user with every single edit the user makes.
When Meta generates content it always produces three variations per platform, each with a different strategic angle. The first variation uses emotional storytelling — it opens with a hook, builds a narrative around the challenge or the process, and ends with a reflection or question. The second variation is direct and technical, written for peers and professionals who want the specifics without the narrative wrapper. The third variation leads with outcomes and results, structured for readers who respond to evidence and achievement. This gives the user real choices rather than one AI output they feel compelled to accept or completely rewrite.
The voice learning system is what makes Meta genuinely useful over time rather than just initially impressive. Every time a user clicks the Refine button and tells Meta to make something more casual, or shorter, or remove the exclamation marks, or add more specific numbers, the system logs that preference and updates the user's voice profile. After two weeks of regular use Meta has learned enough about how the user writes that the first variation it generates is already close to what they would have asked for anyway. The gap between raw generation and approved content shrinks continuously. This is the compounding value of the product — it gets meaningfully better the more you use it, which is what makes retention high and churn low.
What Meta outputs is the full set of platform-specific content cards organized in tabs, the inline refine chat for each individual piece of content, the voice profile update log so users can see what it learned, and the final approved content sent to Snooks for scheduling.

What Happens When You Connect MiniMax, MaxClaw, or OpenClaw
This is where CozyJet becomes genuinely powerful for users who want more than Claude and Gemini as the underlying intelligence. The architecture can be designed from the start to support model routing — a configuration layer that lets users or admins specify which model powers which agent. If MiniMax is connected as an alternative model, its strength in long-context reasoning makes it an excellent fit for the Snooks strategy layer where analyzing thirty days of seeds and analytics simultaneously benefits from a larger context window handling all that data in a single pass without chunking. If a model like MaxClaw or OpenClaw with strong instruction-following and structured output capabilities is connected, it slots naturally into the Meta generation role where the formatting constraints across five different platforms require precise adherence to character limits, structure rules, and variation differentiation. The practical implementation is a model configuration panel in Settings where Pro and Studio subscribers can select their preferred model per agent from a dropdown of connected providers, enter their own API key if they want to use a personal allocation rather than CozyJet's shared pool, and see estimated cost per generation so they can make informed choices. This transforms CozyJet from a product locked to one AI provider into an open marketing studio that routes intelligence where it is most effective, and for users who have existing API relationships with specific providers it means they can leverage those credits directly inside CozyJet rather than paying twice.

The Backend AI Tool That Can Actually Build This With Real Agent Understanding
The most honest answer here is that the tool that comes closest to understanding backend agent architecture deeply enough to build this correctly is a combination of Cursor IDE with Claude Sonnet as the composer model running in Agent mode, specifically because Agent mode in Cursor allows the AI to read your existing files, understand the relationships between them, run terminal commands, check errors, and iterate — it does not just generate code into a void, it builds in the context of what already exists. For CozyJet's backend specifically you would open Cursor, put your complete backend specification in a SPEC.md file at the root of the project, and then in Agent mode tell it to build the project following the spec, starting with the database models and working through to the agents. Because Cursor's agent can read the models it just created before writing the service layer, it avoids the consistency errors that come from generating everything in one shot without cross-file awareness.
The preview side by side workflow that works in practice is running the FastAPI backend locally with uvicorn app.main:app --reload in one terminal, running your Next.js frontend with npm run dev in another, keeping Cursor open in a third window, and using the FastAPI auto-generated docs at localhost:8000/docs as a live interactive preview of every endpoint as it gets built. When Cursor generates a new route you can immediately test it in the Swagger UI without writing any test code manually, see the exact request and response shapes, and if something is wrong you paste the error back into Cursor's agent chat and it fixes it with full context of the surrounding code. This three-pane workflow — Cursor building, terminal running, browser previewing — is the closest thing to a real side-by-side build and preview environment for backend work without a dedicated visual backend builder, and it works particularly well for FastAPI because the automatic documentation generation means the preview updates the moment new code is saved.
